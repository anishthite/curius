How to read this follower graph

Start with one edge: u → v means person u follows person v. Stack those edges into an adjacency matrix A, where Auv is 1 when the edge exists. This is the usual way to measure a directed network Newman 2003.

First count the arrows

Degree is the smallest useful summary. Incoming degree counts followers. Outgoing degree counts people followed.

din(v) = ∑u Auv,  dout(v) = ∑w Avw

In this scrape, the average person has 3.26 observed followers and follows 3.26 people. The graph is sparse: 0.051% of all possible directed pairs without self-follows are present.

Then ask whether arrows come back

Reciprocity checks whether following is mutual. Count an edge u → v as reciprocated when v also follows u.

r = |{(u,v) : Auv = Avu = 1}|m

Here, 9,606 directed edges are reciprocated. That is 46.0% of the edge list.

Components say whether direction matters

A weak component ignores arrow direction. A strong component keeps direction and requires a directed path both ways. The strong-component computation is a direct graph traversal problem; Tarjan gives a linear-time version Tarjan 1972.

Ranking adds one walking reader

PageRank imagines a reader who usually follows an outgoing edge and sometimes jumps anywhere. A person scores high when other high-scoring people follow them. People with no outgoing follows are treated as jumps in the generated numbers. The original paper defines the recursive score for web links Page, Brin, Motwani, and Winograd 1999.

p(v) = 1 − αn + α ∑u→v p(u)dout(u)

Local closure asks whether neighbors know each other

Clustering uses the undirected projection of the follower graph: ignore arrow direction, keep each tie once, and ask how many possible neighbor-neighbor ties are present. Watts and Strogatz use clustering with path length to describe small-world structure Watts and Strogatz 1998.

Cv = 2evkv(kv − 1)

The mean local clustering among people with at least two undirected neighbors is 0.608. The transitivity ratio across all centered triples is 0.124.

The dense middle is a core

A k-core peels away people with fewer than k neighbors in the undirected projection, then repeats. The largest k that keeps a person is that person's core number. A linear-time peeling algorithm is described by Batagelj and Zaversnik 2003.
